\begin{abstract}
Large Language Model serving is bottlenecked by memory capacity for Key-Value (KV) caches. Multi-turn conversations force frequent cache eviction and costly recomputation (the ``cold-start'' problem). We build a three-tier KV cache persistence system (VRAM $\to$ NVMe $\to$ object storage) and evaluate six eviction policies---LRU, a recency heuristic, learned binary classification, static value-density, and a lifetime-normalized space-time density policy---under a statistically rigorous protocol: virtual-clock trace replay, persona-driven synthetic workloads, tier-discounted value accounting, and paired comparisons across 10 seeds with 95\% confidence intervals.

Our evaluation yields four findings. (1)~\emph{LRU is a far stronger baseline than commonly assumed}: under honest accounting it wins both hit rate and dollar value on every capacity-constrained workload we test, with all paired deltas significant. (2)~\emph{Hit rate and value decouple}: on heavy-tailed workloads a simple heuristic retains 97.5\% of LRU's GPU-dollar value while conceding 12 hit-rate points, and policy rankings by hit rate invert under value---our learned classifier beats space-time density by 9 hit-rate points yet delivers \$46/day \emph{less} value. (3)~\emph{Static value-density eviction collapses}: maximizing value-per-byte without bounding sojourn time violates Little's Law dynamics, and our lifetime-normalized space-time objective recovers \$94/day over it (95\% CI [77, 112]). (4)~A break-even analysis shows tiered persistence is latency-positive from $N^{*}{\approx}12$ tokens for 70B-class GQA models but only beyond ${\sim}43$k tokens for 7B MHA models---the KV-bytes-per-FLOP ratio, not model quality, governs deployability. All behavioral findings replicate when replaying real production arrival timestamps and request sizes from the Azure LLM inference trace, where the hit-rate/value inversion is even stronger: a policy conceding 19 hit-rate points delivers \$105/day \emph{more} value. We validate end-to-end with TinyLlama-1.1B on both CPU and GPU: 19$\times$ TTFT improvement on CPU; a T4 context-length sweep whose measured speedup crosses parity between 440 and 1,792 tokens, bracketing the model's predicted crossover; and an in-memory-control ablation showing the storage round-trip adds zero output divergence beyond what any cache-resume path inherently incurs.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The cold-start problem in LLM inference occurs when a user resumes a session whose KV cache has been evicted from VRAM. The system must reprocess the entire prompt history through the transformer's prefill phase, wasting compute and increasing time-to-first-token (TTFT). Existing systems like vLLM~\cite{kwon2023pagedattention} rely on reactive LRU eviction, discarding valuable context without regard to recompute cost.

We explore tiered persistence (VRAM $\to$ NVMe $\to$ Object Storage) combined with predictive eviction. By proactively migrating idle sessions to cheaper tiers, we hypothesized that a learned eviction policy would improve cache hit rates over LRU. \emph{The data falsified this hypothesis}---and diagnosing why became the contribution: eviction under capacity constraints is a packing problem, a cache is a dynamic system governed by Little's Law rather than a static knapsack, and hit rate misprices heavy-tailed workloads whose items have quadratically varying recompute costs.

This paper makes the following contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A \textbf{three-tier KV cache persistence system} with pluggable eviction backends (LRU, heuristic, learned classification, value-density, space-time density) and a \textbf{rigorous evaluation protocol}: virtual-clock replay, persona-driven workloads, replay of the Azure LLM inference production trace, tier-discounted value accounting, and paired multi-seed statistics.
  \item Evidence that \textbf{LRU is a stronger baseline than the learned-caching literature assumes}: it wins both hit rate and dollar value on every constrained workload we test (all paired deltas significant at 95\%).
  \item The demonstration that \textbf{hit rate and cache value decouple} on heavy-tailed workloads---policy rankings invert between the two metrics---so hit rate alone is the wrong target for KV-cache eviction research.
  \item The discovery that \textbf{static value-density optimization collapses cache cardinality} under Little's Law dynamics, and an implemented \textbf{space-time density policy} (an LHD-style objective adapted to quadratic recompute costs) that significantly recovers value over it.
  \item An analytic \textbf{break-even model} for when restoration beats recomputation, showing $N^{*}$ varies by four orders of magnitude with model architecture.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}

\subsection{Self-Attention and the KV Cache}
The self-attention mechanism in transformer-based LLMs attends to all previous tokens. The prefill phase grows quadratically $O(N^2)$ with sequence length $N$, while KV cache memory grows linearly $O(N)$. Inference engines cache key and value tensors in VRAM; however, VRAM is severely constrained, necessitating eviction policies when multiple sessions compete.

\subsection{B\'{e}l\'{a}dy's Algorithm}
The provably optimal offline eviction policy~\cite{belady1966replacement} evicts the item whose next access occurs furthest in the future: $\arg\max_{i} \text{next\_access}(i)$. For uniform-size, uniform-cost items, LRU is an effective online proxy by assuming temporal locality.

\subsection{Weighted Caching}
When items have varying sizes and costs, the uniform assumption fails. The problem maps to a dynamic Fractional Knapsack. As formalized by Young~\cite{young1994caching} and made practical by GreedyDual-Size~\cite{cao1997greedydualsize}, cost-aware eviction must account for value density per byte. LHD~\cite{beckmann2018lhd} further showed that for \emph{dynamic} caches the correct denominator is not size alone but size $\times$ expected lifetime---``hit density.'' In KV caching, ``value'' is the quadratic GPU compute saved by avoiding a cold-start prefill, and ``weight'' is the linear VRAM consumed; our results (\S\ref{sec:eval}) empirically rediscover why the static form of this objective fails and the lifetime-normalized form is required.

\section{Related Work}

\textbf{LLM serving systems.} PagedAttention~\cite{kwon2023pagedattention} partitions the KV cache into fixed-size blocks, eliminating fragmentation and enabling memory sharing. vLLM manages VRAM dynamically but relies on reactive LRU eviction. CachedAttention~\cite{gao2024cachedattention} persists KV caches across multi-turn conversation rounds through a hierarchical cache, though its focus is architectural integration rather than learned eviction policies.

LMCache~\cite{zhong2024lmcache} and CacheBlend~\cite{yao2024cacheblend} focus on cross-request prefix sharing---caching system prompts shared across users. Our work targets single-session persistence: saving user-specific conversational state over long time horizons, where the challenge is predicting which sessions will return.

Mooncake~\cite{qin2025mooncake} proposes a disaggregated KV cache architecture using a tiered hierarchy across nodes. Our work shares the tiered storage motivation but targets the algorithmic eviction policy layer. Where Mooncake asks ``how do we distribute cache across a cluster?'', we ask ``which entries should we keep when capacity is constrained?''

\textbf{Token-level KV compression.} H2O~\cite{zhang2023h2o} and InfiniGen~\cite{lee2024infinigen} evict or offload individual \emph{tokens} within a sequence's KV cache. This is orthogonal to our granularity: we decide which \emph{sessions} to retain across tiers, and token-level compression could be composed with our policies to shrink what we store.

\textbf{Learned cache eviction.} LRB~\cite{song2020lrb} learns to approximate B\'{e}l\'{a}dy's oracle for CDN caching; LHD~\cite{beckmann2018lhd} maximizes hits per unit of space-time occupied. Our work differs in the value model: CDN objects have roughly uniform miss costs, whereas KV cache entries have \emph{quadratically} varying recompute costs, which changes both the correct objective (\S\ref{sec:std}) and, as we show, the correct evaluation metric.

\section{System Design}

Our architecture introduces a \texttt{TieredCacheManager} orchestrating three storage tiers:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Hot Tier (VRAM):} In-memory storage. ${\sim}0.1$ms latency, 50MB capacity.
  \item \textbf{Warm Tier (NVMe SSD):} Filesystem-backed with memory-mapped reads for files $>$10MB. ${\sim}10$ms latency, 150MB capacity.
  \item \textbf{Cold Tier (S3/MinIO):} Object storage with Zstandard compression. ${\sim}100$ms latency, 300MB capacity.
\end{enumerate}

The system exposes three operations: \texttt{save} (serialize and write to hot tier with cascading eviction), \texttt{load} (search tiers in order, deserialize on hit), and \texttt{demote}/\texttt{promote} (explicit tier migration).

\section{Eviction Policy V1: Binary Classification}

Our initial approach formulated eviction as supervised ML: predict $P(\text{resume})$ that a session will be accessed again within 1~hour. We engineered 9~features capturing temporal patterns, access behavior, user history, and session characteristics, and trained Logistic Regression and Gradient Boosted Trees on 7-day persona-driven traces (80/20 split). Test AUC is 0.70 (logistic) and 0.73 (GBT); we use the logistic model in the main study for interpretability---its strongest positive coefficient is the user's historical return rate ($+0.75$), confirming the personas carry learnable signal. Candidate sets are scored in a single batched prediction per eviction sweep.

\textbf{Negative Result:} Under enterprise load, Logistic~V1 achieves \textbf{67.9\%} hit rate [66.8, 69.0] versus LRU's \textbf{79.2\%} [75.9, 82.4]---an 11-point deficit that also translates to \$230/day less GPU value (paired 95\% CI $[-323, -138]$; Table~\ref{tab:results}).

\section{Failure Analysis}

This exposed a fundamental flaw: \textbf{eviction under capacity constraints is not a classification problem; it is a Knapsack variant.} LRU accidentally approximated correct behavior by rotating entries uniformly. Our ML models confidently kept large ``high probability'' sessions that choked capacity, forcing out dozens of smaller entries. The binary criterion $\min P(\text{resume})$ ignores both entry size and variable recompute cost.

% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{Eviction Policies V2 \& V3: Value Density and Space-Time Density}

We reformulated the objective based on weighted caching theory:
\begin{equation}
  \text{Value Density} = \frac{P(\text{resume}) \times \text{RecomputeCost}(N)}{\text{SizeBytes}(N)}
  \label{eq:vd}
\end{equation}

Because prefill scales $O(N^2)$ but memory scales $O(N)$, longer contexts have exponentially higher value density. We also implemented an \textbf{Admission Control Gate}: sessions below a value-density threshold are denied entry to the warm tier.

\subsection{Space-Time Density: Why Static Knapsack Fails}
\label{sec:std}

Our evaluation revealed that static value-density collapses in practice (\S\ref{sec:eval}). A cache is not a static knapsack---it is a dynamic system governed by Little's Law. Each entry occupies \emph{space over time}. The correct objective must account for expected sojourn time $\mathbb{E}[\Delta t]$:
\begin{equation}
  \text{Space-Time Density} = \frac{P(\text{resume}) \times \text{RecomputeCost}(N)}{\text{Size}(N) \times \mathbb{E}[\Delta t]}
  \label{eq:std_eq}
\end{equation}

Under Little's Law, $L = \lambda \times W$, where $L$ is steady-state items, $\lambda$ is arrival rate, and $W$ is average sojourn time. Maximizing $\text{Value}/\text{Byte}$ without bounding $W$ allows large sessions to accumulate unbounded sojourn time, reducing $L$ (cardinality) and collapsing hit rates.

This objective is structurally an adaptation of LHD's hit density~\cite{beckmann2018lhd}---expected hits per unit of size $\times$ lifetime---to the KV-cache setting. The adaptation is non-trivial in two respects: the numerator weights each hit by a \emph{quadratic} recompute cost rather than counting hits uniformly, and $P(\text{resume})$ comes from a learned session-level model rather than age-binned statistics.

\textbf{V3 Implementation.} We estimate $\mathbb{E}[\Delta t]$ per session as an exponential moving average ($\alpha{=}0.3$) of observed inter-access gaps, with a 2-hour prior for sessions with no observed gap. Because an entry idle \emph{longer} than its typical rhythm should expect a longer remaining wait, not a shorter one (the inspection paradox), we score with $\max(\text{EMA gap}, \text{current idle time})$. Candidate sets are re-scored on each eviction sweep.

\section{Experimental Setup}

We evaluated eviction policies using a synthetic workload simulator with three profiles via Poisson arrivals and Log-normal token distributions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Casual:} 50/hr arrival, $\mu{=}512$, $\sigma{=}0.8$, low return probability.
  \item \textbf{Enterprise:} 200/hr, $\mu{=}2048$, $\sigma{=}0.5$, high return probability.
  \item \textbf{Power User:} 80/hr, $\mu{=}8192$, $\sigma{=}1.2$, medium return probability.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{User Personas.} Each simulated user carries a persistent persona---a return-propensity multiplier, a verbosity scale, and a 10-hour diurnal activity window---drawn once and reused across all of that user's sessions. Without personas, per-user features such as historical return rate are i.i.d.\ noise by construction and no predictor can exceed chance (we measured AUC~0.56 under an i.i.d.\ pool vs.\ 0.70 with personas). Conversations grow monotonically: each resume appends new turns to the previously cached context.

\textbf{Virtual Clock.} Traces are replayed on a simulated clock advanced to each event's timestamp. All components---the cache manager, eviction policies, and feature extraction---read time exclusively from this clock. This is essential for validity: replaying a 6-hour trace at wall-clock speed collapses every temporal feature (session age, idle time, hour of day) to near-zero, silently invalidating any policy that reasons about time while leaving order-based policies like LRU intact.

\textbf{Model Configuration.} KV cache sizes are computed using a toy configuration (2~layers, 2~heads, head dimension~32, FP16). A production model (e.g., Llama-2 7B: 32~layers, 32~heads, head dimension~128) would increase sizes by ${\sim}256\times$, shifting absolute capacity dynamics but not relative policy comparisons.

\textbf{Simulation.} 6-hour continuous window; token counts clamped to 8,192 to preserve heavy-tailed distributions.

\textbf{Capacity.} 500MB total (50MB hot + 150MB warm + 300MB cold).

\textbf{GPU Savings.} Each hit is credited with the exact $O(N^2)$ recompute cost of the tokens \emph{actually cached} (not the grown context at resume time), minus the modeled cost of restoring the entry from the tier where it was found (NVMe: 10ms + size/2GB/s; object storage: 100ms + size/500MB/s). Hits are therefore tier-discounted: a cold-tier hit is worth less than a hot-tier hit.

\textbf{Statistics.} Every (policy, workload) cell is run over 10 independent seeds. All policies replay the identical trace per seed, so policy comparisons are \emph{paired}: we report per-seed deltas against LRU with 95\% t-based confidence intervals across seeds.

\subsection{Real-Trace Workload: Azure LLM Inference}
\label{sec:realtrace}

To test external validity beyond synthetic workloads, we additionally replay the Azure LLM inference trace~\cite{azurepublicdataset,stojkovic2025dynamollm}: 27.3M production conversation requests spanning one week of May 2024, each carrying an arrival timestamp and context/generated token counts. Two properties of the trace transfer into our replay verbatim: the \emph{arrival process} (real burstiness and diurnal/weekly load shape; timestamps keep their true UTC hour and weekday on the simulator's Monday-00:00 time axis) and \emph{per-request context sizes} (heavy-tailed: median 928, mean 1{,}632, p95 4{,}943 tokens).

What the trace cannot provide is session identity: for customer privacy it carries no conversation identifiers, and we find linkage is not reconstructable either. A greedy matcher that chains requests by context-token growth (a follow-up turn's context must contain the previous turn's context plus its response) links 87.8\% of requests---but links an indistinguishable 87.8\% on a \emph{permutation control} in which token counts are shuffled across requests, destroying any true chains. At production density (${\sim}60$~req/s over at most 8{,}000 distinct context lengths) growth-chaining is collision-dominated, a caution for any study tempted to reconstruct conversations from these public traces. We therefore impose return behavior using the same persona machinery as the synthetic workloads (enterprise return-window parameters), with resume-time context growth drawn empirically from the window's generated-token distribution. Differences between the synthetic-enterprise and real-trace results are thus attributable to the arrival process and size distribution, which are real; return behavior is held fixed and modeled.

We slice ten 6-hour windows across the week (covering weekday/weekend and peak/trough load) and thin each to 300 session starts per hour by taking every $k$-th request, preserving the arrival shape. The thinning rate is chosen for capacity pressure: real Azure requests are markedly smaller than synthetic enterprise sessions, so at the enterprise arrival rate (200/h) the 500\,MB cache is unconstrained (LRU ${\approx}98\%$ hit rate, no policy differentiation, as with the casual workload), while 300/h restores sustained eviction pressure comparable to the enterprise regime. Each window is one replicate: all six policies replay the identical event list, so deltas remain paired, with windows in the role of seeds. The learned policies reuse the predictor trained on synthetic personas---a zero-shot transfer test. The conversion is implemented in \texttt{benchmarks/azure\_trace\_loader.py}.

\subsection{LRU Is a Stronger Baseline Than Assumed}

Under honest evaluation---virtual-clock replay, cached-token crediting, tier-discounted hits, paired multi-seed statistics---\textbf{LRU wins both hit rate and dollar value on every constrained workload}, and every alternative's paired value deficit is statistically significant. We emphasize this because an earlier version of our own pipeline, which replayed traces at wall-clock speed and credited hits with unclamped resume-time token counts, showed a learned policy \emph{beating} LRU by \$68/day. That result was an artifact: collapsing simulated time zeroed the temporal features the model was trained on, and the accounting credited a different quantity than policies optimized. We report this reversal as a cautionary result for the learned-caching literature: \textbf{evaluation-harness bugs preferentially flatter complex policies}, because simple recency policies are insensitive to broken time.

\subsection{Why Static Knapsack Fails---and Lifetime Normalization Helps}

V2 Value Density drops to 47.9\% hit rate on enterprise and 71.9\% on power-user, the worst of all policies. The Fractional Knapsack formulation is optimal for \emph{static} packing, but a cache is \emph{dynamic} under Little's Law: by maximizing value density, V2 retains massive sessions whose sojourn time is unbounded, starving cache cardinality. Admission control (V2+AC) produces no improvement---under sustained load the cache operates at near-full utilization regardless of filtering.

The space-time correction (V3) directly tests the Little's Law diagnosis: dividing the same value density by $\mathbb{E}[\Delta t]$ recovers \textbf{+\$94/day over V2 on enterprise} (paired 95\% CI $[+77, +112]$) and \textbf{+\$34/day on power-user} $[+20, +49]$, with hit rate improvements of ${\sim}3$ points on both. The theory is validated directionally---lifetime normalization is the right correction---even though V3 still trails LRU. Closing that remaining gap is the open problem.

\subsection{Hit Rate and Value Decouple}

Hit rate remains the wrong lens even where LRU wins. On the power-user workload, the heuristic policy concedes \textbf{11.9 hit-rate points} to LRU (84.2\% vs 96.0\%) yet retains \textbf{97.5\% of its dollar value} ($-\$41$/day of \$1,649): the hits it gives up are disproportionately cheap-to-recompute sessions. Rankings also invert between metrics: Logistic~V1 beats SpaceTime~V3 by 9.1 hit-rate points on power-user, yet delivers \textbf{\$46/day less value} (paired CI $[+25, +68]$ in V3's favor). A researcher selecting policies by hit rate would choose backwards.

\textbf{The correct optimization and evaluation target for KV-cache eviction is not hit rate---it is expected cache value.}

\section{Prototype Validation: TinyLlama}

To validate with a real transformer, we integrated our \texttt{TieredCacheManager} with TinyLlama-1.1B-Chat~(22~layers, 32~Q-heads, 4~KV-heads via GQA, head\_dim=64) on CPU.

\textbf{GQA Compatibility.} Modern models use Grouped Query Attention where KV heads (4) differ from query heads (32). Our serialization must use \texttt{num\_key\_value\_heads} for tensor dimensions---a critical detail for production deployment.

\textbf{Metric definitions.} We report TTFT and end-to-end speedups \emph{separately}. Cold TTFT is a timed prefill-only forward pass---the cost a cache hit actually avoids; warm TTFT is tier load + tensor restore + one forward pass. A tempting shortcut---comparing a cold \texttt{generate()} total (prefill \emph{plus} decode) against warm first-token latency---inflates the apparent speedup ${\sim}2.5\times$ by crediting the warm path with decode time both paths share; we flag this because the conflation is easy to make and appears in informal benchmarks. All GPU timings are bracketed by \texttt{cuda.synchronize()}.

\textbf{CPU results.} Cold prefill of 4,783ms versus a 251ms warm path (16ms tier load + 25ms tensor restore + 210ms first forward pass) is a \textbf{19.1$\times$ TTFT improvement}. End-to-end speedup is 1.43$\times$ because decode-phase speed is independent of cache state.

\textbf{GPU results.} On a T4 the same experiment collapses to parity: prefilling 598 tokens takes ${\sim}$37ms, comparable to the ${\sim}$40ms restore path (median E2E speedup 1.03$\times$ across 9 trials). This is not a failure of the system---it is the break-even model (\S\ref{sec:breakeven}) validated empirically: 598 tokens sits \emph{below} TinyLlama's crossover $N^{*}{\approx}1{,}561$, precisely where persistence should not pay on latency. \S\ref{sec:breakeven} sweeps context length across the crossover.

\textbf{Determinism: storage is exactly as faithful as memory.} At 598 tokens every trial is byte-identical to the cold path on both devices. On short prompts (168 tokens), the resumed path deterministically diverges from the cold path at one near-tie token (both outputs coherent). An ablation attributes the cause: resuming from the \emph{never-serialized, in-memory} cache reproduces the restored-cache output exactly, in every trial---so the storage round-trip (including FP16 quantization under FP32 serving) contributes \emph{nothing}. The divergence stems entirely from computing the resumed token through incremental-decode kernels whose accumulation order differs from the cold path's batched prefill, which can flip near-tie argmax decisions. This is inherent to \emph{any} cache-reuse scheme, including in-memory prefix caching in production servers; tiered persistence adds no divergence beyond it.

\textbf{Cost Model Calibration.} Our simulation cost model (A100 GPU, \$2.50/hr) predicted 57,747ms for a 598-token prefill versus 4,783ms measured on CPU and ${\sim}$37ms on a T4---absolute constants must be calibrated per hardware, while relative policy comparisons remain valid.

\section{When Does Persistence Beat Recompute? A Break-Even Analysis}
\label{sec:breakeven}

The 19$\times$ CPU TTFT speedup above evaporated to parity on a T4, where prefilling 598 tokens takes ${\sim}$37ms---comparable to the restore path itself. Persistence wins on raw latency only beyond a \emph{break-even context length} $N^{*}$, which we can derive analytically and which our GPU measurements land exactly where predicted.

Restoring a persisted cache moves $O(N)$ bytes:
\begin{equation}
  t_{\text{restore}}(N) = \ell_{\text{tier}} + \frac{2 \cdot L \cdot H_{kv} \cdot d \cdot b \cdot N}{B_{\text{tier}}}
\end{equation}
where $\ell$ is tier latency, $B$ bandwidth, $L$ layers, $H_{kv}$ KV heads, $d$ head dimension, $b$ bytes per element. Recomputing the prefill costs $O(N)$ weight FLOPs plus $O(N^2)$ attention:
\begin{equation}
  t_{\text{prefill}}(N) = \frac{2 P N + 4 L H_{q} d N^{2}}{F \cdot \eta}
\end{equation}
with $P$ parameters, $H_q$ \emph{query} heads (GQA shrinks the cache, not the score computation), $F$ peak FLOP/s, and $\eta$ the achievable utilization. Since restore is linear and prefill superlinear, persistence always wins beyond the crossover $N^{*}$.

\section{Discussion \& Future Work}

\textbf{The Metric Mismatch.} Hit rate assumes uniform item value. In KV caching with quadratic recompute costs this is catastrophically wrong: hit-rate gaps of 12 points can correspond to value gaps of 2.5\%, and policy rankings invert between the two metrics. Research and production systems must adopt value-weighted metrics (GPU-seconds saved per decision, discounted by restore cost).

\textbf{Why does LRU win?} Three compounding reasons. First, our workloads exhibit strong temporal locality---the majority of resumes occur within an hour of the previous access---which is precisely LRU's inductive bias, obtained for free without a trained model. Second, value-aware policies pay a structural penalty under Little's Law that lifetime normalization only partially removes. Third, LRU is \emph{robust to evaluation bugs and distribution shift}: it consumes only access order, while learned policies consume nine features whose scales must match training exactly. We suspect published wins for learned eviction that do not control replay-time semantics deserve re-examination.

\textbf{Decision Framework.} (1)~Uniform workloads ($\sigma{=}0.5$): LRU dominates---ML overhead is unjustified. (2)~High-variance ($\sigma{=}1.2$): if the deployment metric is dollar value, simple recency heuristics are within noise of LRU and far cheaper than learned policies; evaluate by \$/Day, never hit rate. (3)~Value-aware eviction must be lifetime-normalized (V3), not static (V2)---but neither yet beats LRU.

\textbf{Limitations.} (1)~The real-trace evaluation replays production arrivals and sizes, but session \emph{return behavior} remains modeled: public LLM inference traces carry no conversation identifiers, and we show linkage is not reconstructable from them (\S\ref{sec:realtrace}). Datasets with hashed conversation identifiers (e.g., WildChat, LMSYS-Chat-1M) could close this gap. (2)~GPU validation used a single T4 at one context length below the crossover; measuring the region \emph{above} $N^{*}$ and on server-class GPUs (A100/H100, where higher FLOPs push $N^{*}$ further out) remains. (3)~Tier restore costs are modeled, not measured on production hardware. (4)~The toy KV geometry preserves relative policy comparisons but not absolute capacity dynamics.

\textbf{Beating LRU.} V3's remaining gap suggests two directions: hybrid policies that default to recency and deviate only for entries in the extreme value tail, where the quadratic payoff dominates; and admission control at the \emph{cold-to-warm promotion} boundary rather than at eviction time, where the Little's Law penalty does not apply.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented a three-tier KV cache persistence system and a statistically rigorous evaluation of six eviction policies across three synthetic workloads, ten seeds, and a replay of the Azure LLM inference production trace, validated end-to-end with TinyLlama-1.1B. Our investigation yielded four insights: (1)~under honest evaluation, LRU beats every learned and value-aware policy we tested on both metrics---and our own earlier harness bugs had shown the opposite, a caution for the learned-caching literature; (2)~hit rate and dollar value decouple on heavy-tailed workloads, to the point of inverting policy rankings---most sharply on the real trace, where the inversion reaches 19 hit-rate points and \$105/day; (3)~static value-density eviction collapses under Little's Law, and lifetime normalization (space-time density, an LHD adaptation) significantly recovers value on synthetic and real workloads alike; (4)~an analytic break-even model shows tiered persistence is latency-positive almost immediately for large GQA models---and our measured T4 crossover (0.62$\times$ at 168 tokens rising through parity to 1.11$\times$ at 1,792) brackets the prediction, while an in-memory-control ablation shows the storage layer is exactly as faithful as keeping the cache in memory. Beating LRU on value remains open; we believe hybrid recency/value-tail policies are the most promising path.
